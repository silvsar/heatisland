Urban Heat Island Effect Intensifies Temperature Differences in Helsinki

By Sara Silvennoinen

This summer, Finland has experienced an exceptionally powerful heatwave, with record-breaking temperatures and unusually prolonged periods of hot weather.

The heatwave was especially intense in urban areas like Helsinki. However, there are also significant differences within the capital area itself.

Data from temperature measurement stations, collected by the Finnish Meteorological Institute, reveal dramatic contrasts between the city center and the outskirts of Helsinki.

The stations in city centre Kamppi and suburban town Haltiala, although only about 12 kilometers apart, show a remarkable temperature difference during the heatwave.

On July 16th, the late-night temperature in Kamppi was as much as 9.29°C higher than in Haltiala. This striking contrast underlines how urban environments, with their dense buildings and asphalt, retain much more heat compared to greener, suburban areas like Haltiala.

Not only were the nighttime temperatures significantly higher in Kamppi, but the rate at which each area cooled also differed greatly.

During the evening and night, Kamppi's temperature dropped much more slowly than Haltiala's. For example, between 18:00 and 03:00, Kamppi cooled by only 5°C, while Haltiala's temperature dropped by 10.6°C. This means Kamppi held onto heat more than two times longer than the suburban area.
